Bits

A computer isn't smart. It can't speak english or think for itself. It's just a bunch of electricity that is either on or off.

Imagine if you and a friend lived in houses across the street. Both of your rooms face eachother. You and your friend want a secret way to communicate with eachother. So you brainstorm and come up with a brilliant idea. You will communitate with your room lights.

The problem is your light only has two states, on and off. You and your friend brainstorm some more and decide to make a secret code. When the light in , you write down "0". When the light is on, you write down "1".

This is how computers "speak". In 1's and 0's. Each 1 or 0 is called a bit.

Nibbles

Ok, so you and your friend have a way to communicate. But just flipping the light on and off a bunch and writing down a bunch of 1's and 0's is hard to understand. You want to be able to read your friends messages more easily then "00101011100001010111".

You decide to devide the 1's and 0's into sets of 4 digits for better readability.

So "00101011100001010111" becomes "0010 1011 1000 0101 0111". Much better.

Not long after that, as you sit on your couch being a potato, you are struck with an idea: what if each slot represented a bigger number and the 1's and 0's just told you whether that slot was active.

After more brainstorming you decide on this:
0 | 0 | 0 | 0
8 | 4 | 2 | 1

Now you can represent bigger numbers. Here are some expamples to really drive home the idea:
0001 -> 1
0010 -> 2
0101 -> 5
1001 -> 9
1000 -> 8

You call these sets of 4 bits, Nibbles. Make sense?

Hex

Now reading strings 1's and 0's is just not doable so you and your buddy come up with a system to make your nibbles better. Numbers 10-15 are replaced with letters A-F.

Like this:
1010 -> 10 -> A
1011 -> 11 -> B
1100 -> 12 -> C
1101 -> 13 -> D
1110 -> 14 -> E
1111 -> 15 -> F

Behold, expamples:
1001 0100 1011 0011 -> 9 6 B 3
1111 0010 0001 0100 -> F 2 1 6
0001 0001 1011 0000 -> 1 1 B 0

So much more simpler to read.

Bytes

Now this is where it gets a bit... more complicated.

You don't wanna be stuck with just one nibble to express your self, right? So, you compine two nibbles to create a byte.

A byte: 11111111 -> 255 -> FF

The tricky thing here is understanding that two nibbles does not equal two 15's (which would make sense because the hex is FF).
It keeps going up exponentially:
128 | 64 | 32 | 16 | 8 | 4 | 2 | 1

If you have multiple bytes this pattern continues.
Here is two bytes:
32,768 ∣ 16,384 ∣ 8,192 ∣ 4,096 ∣ 2,048 ∣ 1,024 ∣ 512 ∣ 256 ∣ 128 ∣ 64 ∣ 32 ∣ 16 ∣ 8 ∣ 4 ∣ 2 ∣ 1

Here are some more explamples:
1 Byte | Binary: 10001001 -> Hex: 89 -> Number: 137

3 Bytes | Binary: 10000101 10100101 00101011 -> Hex: 87A94B -> Number: 8,932,147

4 Bytes | Binary: 10001111 01001101 11010101 01001001 -> Hex: 8F4D5A49 -> Number: 2,389,204,745
