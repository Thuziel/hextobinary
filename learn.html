Bits

A computer isn't smart. It can't speak English or think for itself. It's just a bunch of electricity that is either on or off.

Imagine if you and a friend lived in houses across the street. Both of your rooms face each other. You and your friend want a secret way to communicate with each other, so you come up with a brilliant idea: you will use your room lights.

The problem is your light only has two states: on and off. You decide to make a secret code. When the light is off, you write down "0". When the light is on, you write down "1".

This is how computers "speak"—in 1's and 0's. Each 1 or 0 is called a bit.

Nibbles

Now, just flipping the light on and off and writing down a bunch of 1's and 0's can be hard to understand. To make it easier, you decide to group the 1's and 0's into sets of 4 digits for better readability.

So "00101011100001010111" becomes "0010 1011 1000 0101 0111". Much better.

Then, you come up with an idea: what if each slot represented a bigger number, and the 1's and 0's just told you whether that slot was active?

Here's how it works:
0 | 0 | 0 | 0
8 | 4 | 2 | 1

Now you can represent bigger numbers. Here are some examples:
0001 -> 1
0010 -> 2
0101 -> 5
1001 -> 9
1000 -> 8

You call these sets of 4 bits Nibbles. Make sense?

Hex

Reading long strings of 1's and 0's is challenging, so you and your friend develop a system to simplify nibbles. Numbers 10-15 are replaced with letters A-F.

For example:
1010 -> 10 -> A
1011 -> 11 -> B
1100 -> 12 -> C
1101 -> 13 -> D
1110 -> 14 -> E
1111 -> 15 -> F

Now, hexadecimal makes it easier to read binary data:
1001 0100 1011 0011 -> 9 6 B 3
1111 0010 0001 0100 -> F 2 1 6
0001 0001 1011 0000 -> 1 1 B 0

Bytes

Now it gets a bit more complicated. Instead of just one nibble, you combine two nibbles to create a byte.

A byte: 11111111 -> 255 -> FF

Remember that two nibbles don't equal two 15's. The value increases exponentially:
128 | 64 | 32 | 16 | 8 | 4 | 2 | 1

If you have multiple bytes, this pattern continues:
32,768 ∣ 16,384 ∣ 8,192 ∣ 4,096 ∣ 2,048 ∣ 1,024 ∣ 512 ∣ 256 ∣ 128 ∣ 64 ∣ 32 ∣ 16 ∣ 8 ∣ 4 ∣ 2 ∣ 1

Example:
4 Bytes
Binary: 10001111 01001101 11010101 01001001
Hex: 8F4D5A49
Number: 2,389,204,745

The End
